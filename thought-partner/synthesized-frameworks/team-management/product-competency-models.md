# Product Competency Models

## Purpose

Product competency models define the skills, knowledge, and behaviors that effective product managers need to succeed. They help with hiring, performance evaluation, career development, and identifying skill gaps. Different models emphasize different aspects of product management based on company stage, role level, and organizational needs.

## When to Use

- Hiring product managers: Defining what to look for in candidates
- Performance evaluation: Assessing PM performance and identifying growth areas
- Career development: Creating development plans and career paths
- Team assessment: Understanding team strengths and gaps
- Role definition: Clarifying what PMs should be doing
- Skill gap analysis: Identifying what skills team needs to develop

## Key Components/Steps

### PM Mission (Core Purpose)

**Deliver value to customers in a way that grows the business and is hard to copy.**

### Evaluation Criteria

Three key areas for evaluating PM performance:

1. **Insight**: How well can you gather insights & create a plan for delivering on the PM Mission?
2. **Execution**: How well can you lead a team to execute against your plan?
3. **Impact**: 
   - **Business**: Actually achieving the PM mission
   - **Organizational**: Making the organization better

### Core Competency Areas

#### 1. Product Execution
**How well can you deliver high-quality products effectively and efficiently?**

- **Roadmapping**: Prioritizes and sequences work to balance impact, effort, and dependencies
- **Project Management**: Drives clear plans and delivers on time
- **Product Definition**: Scopes features clearly and concisely for teams and partners
- **Technical Collaboration**: Works with engineering to spec features & navigate trade-offs
- **XF Collaboration**: Aligns with cross-functional partners on goals, roles, and execution
- **Quality Assurance**: Ships reliable, polished features that minimize tech debt
- **Analytics**: Defines and measures success metrics
- **Follow-through**: Owns the release and iteration post-launch

#### 2. Insight (Customer & Market Understanding)
**How well can you understand your customers, your market, and your business?**

- **User Knowledge**: Knows who users are and what they need
- **Market Knowledge**: Tracks industry trends, competition, and shifts
- **Business Knowledge**: Understands the business model and how product drives value
- **Data Fluency**: Uses data to find insights, make calls, and measure impact
- **Discovery Systems**: Develops systems to facilitate discovery for the team

#### 3. Product Sense
**How well do you make the right product decisions under ambiguity?**

- **Cognitive Empathy**: Anticipates how users think and behave
- **Creativity**: Generates smart, simple, novel solutions
- **Domain Knowledge**: Applies deep context to guide product decisions
- **Prioritization**: Focuses teams on what matters most
- **Design Thinking**: Co-creates intuitive, elegant user experiences
- **Product Judgment**: Knows what great looks like and holds the bar

#### 4. Product Vision & Strategy
**How well can you create and communicate a compelling product vision and strategy?**

- **Strategic Thinking**: Develops long-term vision and strategy
- **Product Vision**: Creates compelling vision for future state
- **Product Strategy**: Develops plan to achieve vision while satisfying business objectives
- **Commercial Sense**: Understands business model, pricing, monetization
- **Critical Thinking**: Evaluates options and makes sound decisions

#### 5. Influence & Leadership
**How well can you rally people around the team's work?**

- **Influential Communication**: Adapts messaging to influence outcomes, shapes narratives
- **Stakeholder Management**: Builds deep, trusting partnerships, manages up and across
- **Principled Negotiation**: Navigates trade-offs, drives aligned win-win decisions
- **Leading Without Authority**: Leads complex work across teams, shapes culture
- **Team Energy & Focus**: Keeps teams motivated and focused, shapes team norms

#### 6. People/PM Management (For Leaders)
**How well can you develop and manage other PMs?**

- **PM Management**: Hires, develops, and manages product managers
- **Coaching**: Provides guidance and development for PMs
- **Team Management**: Builds and leads product teams
- **Recruiting**: Identifies and attracts top PM talent

   - Product Sense

3. **Product Strategy**
   - Product Vision & Strategy

### Competency by Company Stage

**Early Stage (0-50 employees)**
- Heavy emphasis on execution and doing
- Need to wear many hats
- Less formal processes

**Growth Stage (50-500 employees)**
- Balance of execution and strategy
- More structured processes
- Cross-functional collaboration critical

**Scale Stage (500+ employees)**
- Heavy emphasis on strategy and influence
- Managing through others
- Organizational impact

### Competency by Level

**Associate PM / PM**
- Focus on execution and learning
- Works on well-defined problems
- Strong in one or two competency areas

**Senior PM**
- Strong across multiple competency areas
- Works on ambiguous problems
- Influences without authority

**Lead PM / Principal PM**
- Excels across all competency areas
- Sets direction and strategy
- Shapes organizational approach

**Group PM / Director**
- Manages other PMs
- Sets product vision and strategy
- Organizational impact and leadership

## Key Principles

- **PM Mission**: Deliver value to customers in a way that grows the business and is hard to copy
- **Three evaluation criteria**: Insight, Execution, Impact (business + organizational)
- **Competency areas evolve**: Different skills needed at different stages and levels
- **T-shaped skills**: Deep in some areas, broad across all areas
- **No one is great at everything**: Understand gaps and build teams that complement
- **Competencies build on each other**: Execution enables strategy, insight enables execution
- **Context matters**: Competencies needed vary by company stage, role level, domain

## Common Pitfalls

- **Focusing only on execution**: Ignoring strategy, vision, and influence
- **Focusing only on strategy**: Ignoring execution and getting things done
- **Not adapting to company stage**: Using wrong competencies for stage
- **Not developing breadth**: Too deep in one area, weak in others
- **Ignoring influence skills**: Thinking technical skills are enough
- **Not evolving with level**: Not developing leadership skills as you advance
- **Not understanding gaps**: Not knowing what you're weak at
- **Not building complementary teams**: Hiring people just like you
- **Ignoring organizational impact**: Only focusing on product impact
- **Not developing others**: Not building PM management skills as you advance
